Step 1 of 6
What kind of conflict is your team facing?
Identifying the type of conflict is the first step toward resolving it effectively.
Step 2 of 6
How did you try to communicate?
Check every method your team has already used to address this conflict. Being proactive about communication is a core engineering teamwork skill.
In professional engineering teams, documenting your communication attempts is just as important as making them. This step helps you build that habit.
Before documenting a conflict, try reaching out first. A quick text or message is often all it takes. Engineers resolve most team issues through direct, respectful communication before escalating.
Step 3 of 6
Describe the situation
Be factual and specific. Focus on observable behaviors and impacts, not assumptions about intentions.
Engineering tip: Good conflict documentation uses facts, not feelings. Instead of "they don't care," write "they have not responded to 3 messages over 5 days."
What specifically happened? (facts only)
Describe the observable situation. Dates, actions, and specific behaviors are more useful than general impressions.
How has this affected the team and the work?
Describe the impact on your project, your teammates, and your team dynamic.
Step 4 of 6
Choose your resolution strategies
Select the approaches you plan to use (or have already used) to resolve this conflict. These are real techniques used by professional engineering teams.
Direct conversation
Have a private, respectful one-on-one conversation to understand what's going on. Lead with curiosity, not accusation.
Redistribute and reassign tasks
Formally reassign tasks with clear individual ownership and a specific deadline — documented in a shared space.
Establish regular check-ins
Schedule brief recurring team check-ins (even 10 minutes) to surface blockers early before they become bigger problems.
Put expectations in writing
Write down what each person agreed to do and by when — in a shared doc or message thread — so there is no ambiguity.
Involve a neutral third party
Ask another trusted teammate to help mediate, or seek guidance from a GTA or instructor if the conflict can't be resolved internally.
Revisit the team contract
Go back to the team contract you signed at the start of the semester. Use it as an objective reference point for expected behavior.
Switch to asynchronous work
Restructure the remaining work so teammates can contribute independently and on their own schedule, reducing coordination friction.
Step 5 of 6
Reflect on what you're learning
This is the most important step. Engineering is a team sport — how you navigate conflict is a skill you'll use your entire career.
There are no right or wrong answers here. Honest reflection is what turns a difficult experience into a real learning opportunity.
What could you or your team have done earlier to prevent this conflict?
Think about communication habits, task clarity, or team norms that might have helped.
What did you learn about yourself as a team member through this experience?
Think about how you responded, what was hard, and what you'd do differently.
What is one thing you will do differently in your next team project?
Be specific — a concrete commitment is more useful than a vague intention.
